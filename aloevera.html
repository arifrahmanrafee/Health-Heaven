Aloe Vera

Scientic name : Aloe vera (L.)

A. vera from Liliaceae is known as ‘ghritkumari’ in Bangladesh and India, and ‘Aloe vera’ worldwide. The leaves, mainly, of A. vera have great medicinal importance as they have active chemical compounds such as saponins, essential amino acids (e.g., cysteine, alanin, arginine, and histidine); anthraquinones (e.g., aloetic acid, aloin A and B (or collec-tively known as barbaloin), anthracine, anthranon, emodin, etc.); enzymes (e.g., alkaline phosphotase, amylase, catalase, cellulase, cyclooxidase, and lipase); hormones (e.g., auxin and gibberllins); chromones, lignin, minerals, salicylic acid, sterols, carbohydrates (e.g., lignins and sugars); dietary bers, protein, organic acids, lipids, and vitamins. Health Benefits

  • Aloe vera gel is widely used for its soothing and cooling effects on burns and sunburns. It helps reduce redness, inflammation, and pain while promoting healing.
  • Aloe vera juice has a natural laxative effect, which can help relieve constipation. However, it should be used sparingly to avoid dependency or adverse effects.
  • Aloe vera contains compounds like salicylic acid that have anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties, which can help reduce inflammation and pain from various conditions, including arthritis and muscle pain.
  • The gel contains vitamins C and E, which help improve skin elasticity, reduce wrinkles, and promote a youthful appearance.

Disease Prevention

  • Aloe vera can help reduce cholesterol levels and improve blood circulation, which may contribute to better heart health. Its anti-inflammatory properties also support cardiovascular health.
  • Aloe vera may help lower blood sugar levels and improve insulin sensitivity, making it beneficial for people with type 2 diabetes. However, it should be used under medical supervision.
  • Some studies suggest that aloe vera may have anti-cancer properties due to its antioxidants and immune-boosting effects. However, more research is needed in this area.
